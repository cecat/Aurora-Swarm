## Claude Opus 4.7

The filter you've now described — components with context-dependent semantic state, heterogeneous populations, behavior that isn't closed-form but is encoded somewhere as scientific knowledge, and quantitative ground truth for validation — narrows the landscape sharply. Social/policy is out because ground truth is soft. Pure physics is out because behavior is closed-form and coded simulation wins. The sweet spot is biology-adjacent systems where component-level decision rules live in the literature rather than in equations, and where the population behavior is routinely measured. Four candidates I'd push hard on:

### Cell-level agents in multicellular dynamics.

Each agent is one cell with persistent state: cell type, position, neighbors, lineage history, current signaling/metabolic context. Behavior — divide, differentiate, migrate, senesce, apoptose, secrete — is context-dependent and non-deterministic, influenced by the cell's own state plus signals from neighbors. The substantive argument is that existing approaches (Boolean networks, Potts/CompuCell3D, PhysiCell) reduce cell fate to small state vectors or hand-coded rules, and everyone in the field knows the rules are richer than that — they live in decades of cell-biology literature. An LLM-per-cell, grounded on cell-type-specific knowledge, lets the rules be as rich as the literature that encodes them. Quantitative validation is outstanding: single-cell RNA-seq gives you joint distributions over cell states; spatial transcriptomics (MERFISH, Visium HD, CosMx) gives you spatial organization; live imaging + lineage tracing gives you trajectories. Concrete systems where 10k–100k cells is the natural scale: gastruloid/organoid development (predict cell-type proportions and spatial arrangements vs observed atlases), tumor heterogeneity under drug pressure (predict subpopulation dynamics vs longitudinal scRNA-seq), wound healing or salamander limb regeneration (predict blastema composition and dynamics). The 10k–1M scale is load-bearing because population-level emergent properties (patterning, niche formation, collective migration) only appear above threshold densities and require enough independent lineages to get statistics.

### Microbial consortia with genome-encoded capability.

Each agent is a strain with state derived from its genome: metabolic capabilities (which substrates it can consume, which it produces), signaling repertoire (quorum sensing, interference compounds), environmental tolerances. Behavior is context-dependent in a strong sense — a strain's growth rate depends on what neighbors are producing, consuming, and signaling. The literature-grounded part is that each strain's capabilities are encoded in genome annotations plus KEGG/MetaCyc pathway knowledge plus a lot of contextual experimental papers. Conventional community models (MICOM, COMETS) use flux-balance analysis, which is powerful but context-blind. LLM-per-strain could reason about regulation, stress responses, niche construction in ways FBA can't. Quantitative ground truth is plentiful: 16S and shotgun metagenomics from gut, soil, marine, wastewater, and bioreactor systems; defined co-culture experiments; synthetic consortia. Scale matters because community behavior depends on diversity — 10 strains is a toy, 10k strains is a real soil or gut community.

### Reaction-network agents for complex mixture chemistry.

Each agent is a chemical species with semantic state (structure, reactivity profile, known reactions). Behavior is stochastic reaction with other species, conditioned on environment (solvent, temperature, light, catalysts present). The systems where this matters and traditional quantum-chem methods struggle because of combinatorial explosion: prebiotic chemistry (Miller-Urey-style mixtures evolving over millions of steps), atmospheric chemistry (tens of thousands of species tracked in air-quality models), combustion of complex fuels, industrial catalyst deactivation in complex feeds. You'd pair the LLM swarm with a fast reactive-force-field or DFT oracle for per-step validation; the LLM swarm's job is to propose plausible reaction hypotheses and prune the combinatorial explosion. Quantitative validation: mass-spec product distributions, rate measurements, isotope labeling studies.

### Protein variants / functional landscape agents.

Each agent is a sequence variant (point mutations, insertions, deletions, domain swaps) with state = predicted structure + annotated function + evolutionary context. Agents reason about consequences of mutations for activity, stability, specificity, and epistasis with other variants. This is the one with the cleanest ground truth: deep mutational scans now cover tens of thousands of variants per protein with quantitative fitness/activity readouts, and the field is growing fast. The existing specialized tools (ESM variants, AlphaMissense, DDG predictors) are excellent at certain tasks but poor at integrative reasoning about why a mutation matters in a pathway or clinical context. LLM agents with per-variant persistent state could explore epistasis landscapes in ways pairwise predictors can't.

### Caveats and Comments

A more honest note on where the 10k scale actually earns its keep. For combinatorial search and mechanism hypothesis generation, scale buys coverage — you're sampling a combinatorial space. For cell/microbial/reaction-network simulations, scale buys emergent collective behavior that only appears with enough independent components in contact, and enough statistics to compare distributions rather than single trajectories against experimental data. The latter is really the criterion: if your validation plan is "the simulation produces distribution P and the experiment gives distribution Q, and we score KL or Wasserstein," you need enough agents to estimate P well. 10k is typically sufficient; 1M gives you access to rare cell states / rare reaction products / rare variants.

A caveat you'll already know: in each of these, the LLM-per-component is only interesting if it genuinely carries context the component-level specialized predictor misses. If the cell's behavior reduces to a transition matrix, Markov models win. If the strain's behavior reduces to its flux-balance solution, MICOM wins. The value-add is integration across heterogeneous information types where no single predictor captures the joint context. That's the argument you'd have to make crisply in any proposal.

Of the four, cell-level tissue dynamics is the one I'd bet on if the goal is a publishable demonstration that lands in a top biology venue — the data ecosystem is the best, the community's already primed by the "foundation model for cells" work (Cell2Sentence, scGPT, Geneformer), and extending from single-cell representation learning to multicellular LLM-agent simulation is a clear next step nobody has credibly done at NAS-swarm scale yet.
